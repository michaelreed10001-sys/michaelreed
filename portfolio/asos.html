Operational Design Principles

Four pillars that guided every decision

Before discovery began, the team aligned on a set of non-negotiable design principles. These acted as guard rails throughout every sprint — ensuring we never drifted from user value.

1. Fit transparency

Surface true size data from purchase history, not just brand-supplied charts that vary wildly between suppliers.

2. Frictionless discovery

Place fit help exactly where users already look — on listings, product pages, and at the moment of commitment.

3. Data-backed confidence

Recommendations powered by real purchase-and-return data, not guesswork. Every suggestion is explainable.

4. Cross-brand consistency

A single saved profile that translates intelligently across ASOS's 2,000+ brand catalogue — once set up, always useful.

Helping customers find clothes that fit — before they find the product

Sizing problems start at search, not the product page. We redesigned the discovery experience so fit preferences travel with the user throughout their shopping journey — from first search query to final confirmation.

Search with fit intent

When a user types "midi dress" with a saved profile, autocomplete surfaces "midi dresses that fit your size" as a suggested query. Search results are pre-filtered to stock their saved size where available.

Fit-first filtering on PLP

A new "Fit" filter panel surfaces four options: True to size, Runs small, Runs large, Petite/Tall/Plus. Filters are informed by aggregated purchase-and-return data, not just brand labels. Fit filter state persists across sessions.

Fit badges on product cards

PLP product thumbnails display a subtle "Fits true to size" or "Order up" badge pulled from return data. A/B testing showed an 18% increase in correct-size purchases among users who saw the badge before clicking through.

Personalised size availability

For logged-in users with a saved profile, product cards in their predicted size are marked with a green availability indicator. Out-of-stock sizes in their profile trigger a back-in-stock notification prompt.

Fit alerts & notifications

Users who saved items but didn't buy receive fit-specific nudges: "This runs small — order one size up" or "Your size is back in stock." Push notification CTR improved by 31% vs generic back-in-stock alerts.

Style history & fit memory

The "My Fit" profile page shows a purchase history annotated with fit outcomes (kept, returned for size, exchanged). This data feeds the recommendation model and also gives users a personal reference for repeat purchases.

Discovery redesign impact

In the 6-week post-launch measurement window, the fit filter was used by 38% of sessions — making it the third most-used filter after price and category. PLP-to-PDP click-through rate improved by 22% for users with an active fit profile, and average order value increased £8.40 as customers bought fewer speculative duplicate sizes.

Brainstorming Workshop

A full-day ideation session with the cross-functional team

Before committing to any solution direction, we ran a structured full-day ideation workshop. The goal was to diverge widely before converging — ensuring we hadn't anchored too early on the obvious solution.

Why we ran this workshop before designing anything

After 5 weeks of research, we had rich data — but no solution. The risk at this stage is that the design lead jumps straight to wireframes, biased by their own mental models. A structured workshop externalises the thinking and pools the collective intelligence of the cross-functional team.

The workshop was designed around the Double Diamond principle: we needed a genuine diverge phase before any converge decisions were made. Too many design projects skip divergence — the workshop protected against that.

Workshop design principles

Diverge before converge. No idea was too wild in the morning. Evaluation and prioritisation only happened in the afternoon.

Silent first, vocal second. Every generative activity was done silently before any sharing — preventing anchoring bias and louder voices dominating.

Votes not vibes. All shortlisting was done through structured dot voting, not consensus discussion — protecting against groupthink.

User data always in the room. Printed research insights and user quotes were visible throughout — keeping the team anchored to validated problems, not hypotheticals.

How Might We — session prompts

"How might we help ASOS shoppers feel confident they're buying the right size — without asking them to measure themselves every time?"

"How might we turn ASOS's unique return data into a fit signal that customers can actually trust?"

"How might we make fit help feel native to the shopping flow — not a separate tool that interrupts the experience?"

These three prompts were displayed throughout the day. Every sketch and concept had to trace back to at least one of them.

Crazy 8s Workshop

64 concepts in 30 minutes

The Crazy 8s exercise was the engine of divergence. 8 participants, each producing 8 sketches in 8 minutes — a volume of ideas that would be impossible to generate in a conventional discussion. The constraint of time is the point: it bypasses self-censorship and forces raw intuition onto paper.

8 Participants
8 Sketches each
1min Per sketch
64 Ideas total
12 Advanced to prototype

How it ran — step by step

The exercise was timed strictly. No exceptions. The pressure is deliberate — when participants can't second-guess themselves, surprising ideas emerge. Three facilitator rules were enforced: no explaining, no erasing, no judging.

Pre-brief (5 min)

Facilitator shared the three HMW prompts and reminded participants there were no wrong answers. Phones face-down. No laptops. Printed stimulus cards with user quotes on each desk.

8 rounds × 1 minute

Each round was timed with an audible beep. Participants sketched one concept per panel — a screen, a flow, a feature idea. No refinement, no perfection. The goal was quantity, not quality.

Photo capture

At the end of each round, sketches were photographed and added to a shared Miro board in real time, visible to the room on the main screen — creating a live wall of ideas.

Dot voting (10 min)

Each participant was given 5 dot stickers and 10 minutes to vote silently. Votes could be stacked on a single idea. No discussion until all dots were placed. The facilitator counted and photographed results.

Group debrief (15 min)

Top-voted sketches were discussed openly. Authors explained their thinking briefly. The group identified convergence patterns — concepts that appeared independently across multiple participants were flagged as strong signals.

Affinity Clustering

Finding signal in 64 ideas

After Crazy 8s, we had 64 rough concepts spread across a Miro board. Affinity clustering turned that volume of ideas into three actionable themes that aligned both user needs and business goals.

What is affinity clustering and why does it work?

Affinity clustering (also called affinity mapping or affinity diagramming) is a bottom-up method for finding natural groupings in qualitative data. Rather than imposing categories top-down, the team moves ideas freely until emergent patterns become visible. This preserves nuance and prevents premature closure on obvious groupings.

The technique originated in the Jiro Kawakita (KJ) method from 1960s Japan and has been a staple of design thinking since IDEO popularised it in the 1990s. For the ASOS workshop, we ran a digitised version using Miro — enabling remote asynchronous participation alongside the in-room session.

Why we used Miro: Digital stickies meant Engineering team members who couldn't attend in person could still contribute clustering decisions asynchronously before the next day's review.

Rules we followed

1

No talking during grouping. Participants moved stickies silently for 20 minutes. Forcing non-verbal communication prevented dominant voices from steering clusters prematurely.

2

Duplicate contested stickies. When an idea fit two clusters, we duplicated it into both. Preserving cross-cluster ideas prevented loss of nuanced concepts.

3

Name clusters after grouping, not before. Labels emerged from the content — not the other way around. This prevented premature framing.

4

Heat-map vote on cluster priority. After naming, each cluster received a 1–5 strategic value rating from every participant. Weighted average determined which themes entered MoSCoW as priority areas.

Three Amigos Sessions

Bridging design, engineering & QA before a line of code was written

Three Amigos is a structured refinement practice where Design, Engineering, and QA review each feature together before development begins. The goal: align on acceptance criteria, surface technical constraints, and prevent expensive late-stage rework.

Why Three Amigos — and why it mattered on this project

The Three Amigos practice emerged from Behaviour-Driven Development (BDD) but has become a cross-functional best practice beyond engineering teams. The premise is simple: the three most likely causes of delivery failure are misunderstood requirements (Design), hidden complexity (Engineering), and untested edge cases (QA). Getting all three perspectives in the same room before any sprint work begins prevents the most expensive kind of rework.

On the ASOS Fit Assist project, we ran Three Amigos sessions in weeks 5–7 — intentionally after Crazy 8s and affinity clustering had shaped the direction, but before any designs were locked. This meant the conversations shaped the design, rather than reacting to it.

"Starting Three Amigos in week 5 saved us two rounds of scope change. We surfaced the ML model constraint before we'd built a single screen around it."

— Lead Product Designer, ASOS Fit Assist

How each session was structured

45–75 min time-boxed sessions

Each session covered one or two closely related features. The hard time limit kept discussions focused and prevented scope creep.

Design presents prototype first

Design always walked through the Figma prototype at the start. No requirements doc — just the living design. This anchored discussion in real user experience.

QA writes AC in session

QA documented acceptance criteria on a shared Confluence page in real time — meaning output was always immediately accessible to the full team.

Engineering flags blockers immediately

Any technical constraint that required a design change was flagged with a priority level (blocker / nice-to-know). Design iterated within the same session where possible.

Design

Owns the user experience intent. Presents prototypes and interaction specs. Flags edge cases from usability research.

Brings: Figma prototype · User flows · Interaction notes
+

Engineering

Owns technical feasibility and system constraints. Raises API limitations, performance concerns, and implementation complexity.

Brings: API specs · Data model · Complexity estimate
+

Owns quality and acceptance. Writes test scenarios from requirements. Proactively identifies edge cases that need design decisions.

MoSCoW Prioritisation

Aligning the team on what ships and when

MoSCoW was the bridge between ideation and delivery. After the workshop generated 64 ideas and Three Amigos validated feasibility, we needed a shared framework to align the full team — design, engineering, product, and stakeholders — on a single prioritised backlog. The exercise was run as a 90-minute facilitated session with eight participants.

What is MoSCoW?

A prioritisation framework built for alignment under time pressure

MoSCoW (Must have, Should have, Could have, Won't have now) is a structured technique for aligning cross-functional teams on feature priority without getting stuck in endless negotiation. Each item gets a single, unambiguous label that reflects business impact, user value, and technical readiness.

The method is especially effective when time and resource constraints are real — forcing honest trade-offs rather than optimistic wishlists. For the ASOS Fit Assist project, it was the bridge between 64 raw workshop ideas and a lean, deliverable sprint backlog.

Key principle: MoSCoW only works when "Must" is genuinely constrained. We enforced a rule — Must haves could not exceed 40% of all features. If everything is critical, nothing is. This forced real trade-offs.
Must Have

Non-negotiable. Without these the product fails. Always delivered first, defining the minimum viable scope.

Should Have

Important but not critical. Expected in the final product — a temporary workaround could exist, but isn't desirable.

Could Have

Nice-to-have. Improves experience but won't be missed if dropped. Included only if time and capacity allow.

Won't Have (Now)

Explicitly out of scope this cycle. Not permanently rejected — parked to protect delivery focus and revisited later.

Reflections

What I'd do differently

Earlier engineering alignment

Three Amigos sessions were valuable — but starting them in week 1 rather than week 5 would have saved two rounds of scope adjustment mid-design.

Broader user research segments

Our research skewed urban and female. A more representative sample, especially for international sizing and menswear, would strengthen the cross-brand model.

Invest earlier in data quality

Return reason data was messier than expected — free-text fields rather than structured tags. Advocating for data taxonomy investment earlier would have unlocked richer fit signals.
